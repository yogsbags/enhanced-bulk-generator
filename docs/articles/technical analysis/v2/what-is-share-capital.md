In the world of investing, **share capital** is often the first line item on a balance sheet, yet it remains one of the most misunderstood concepts. It isn't just "money in the bank"; it represents the permanent finance provided by owners—the skin in the game that protects creditors and fuels growth. Whether you are analyzing a massive conglomerate like Reliance Industries or evaluating a fresh IPO like Hero Fincorp, understanding the nuances of share capital—how it is raised, how it is altered, and how it is taxed—is fundamental to assessing a company's financial health and shareholder value.

## The Anatomy of Share Capital

Share capital is not a single static number. It functions more like a funnel, starting with a regulatory ceiling and narrowing down to the actual money in the company's account. As per the **Companies Act, 2013**, this hierarchy is rigid and must be disclosed in the Memorandum of Association (MoA).

### 1. Authorized Share Capital
This is the maximum capital a company is legally allowed to raise. Think of it as a credit limit on a credit card—you might not use it all, but you cannot exceed it without regulatory approval.
*   **Regulatory Context:** To increase this limit, a company must pay a fee to the Registrar of Companies (ROC) and stamp duty. As of November 2025, stamp duty on authorized capital varies by state (e.g., Maharashtra and Delhi have different slab rates).
*   **Investor Relevance:** If a company's paid-up capital is close to its authorized capital, it may need shareholder approval to raise more funds, often signaling upcoming dilution.

### 2. Issued Share Capital
This is the portion of authorized capital that the company actually offers to investors. It can never exceed the authorized capital.
*   **Example:** If a company has ₹10 crore Authorized Capital but only needs ₹5 crore now, it will issue shares worth ₹5 crore.

### 3. Subscribed Share Capital
This represents the part of issued capital that investors have agreed to buy. In a successful IPO, this figure matches the Issued Capital. If an IPO is undersubscribed (e.g., only 90% bids received), the Subscribed Capital will be lower than Issued Capital.

### 4. Called-Up and Paid-Up Share Capital
*   **Called-Up:** The amount the company has asked shareholders to pay. (Rarely used today as most shares are fully paid upfront).
*   **Paid-Up Capital:** The actual money received from shareholders. This is the figure used to calculate key metrics like **Earnings Per Share (EPS)** and **Return on Equity (ROE)**.

| Capital Type | Definition | Practical Implication |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Authorized** | Maximum allowed limit | Ceiling for fundraising; requires fee to increase. |
| **Issued** | Offered to public | Shows intent to raise funds. |
| **Subscribed** | Agreed to be bought | Indicates investor demand. |
| **Paid-up** | Actually received | **The real money** used for business operations. |

## Types of Share Capital in India

Under **Section 43 of the Companies Act, 2013**, share capital is broadly classified into two categories. Understanding the difference is vital for risk management.

### 1. Equity Share Capital
Equity shareholders are the true owners. They bear the highest risk and enjoy the highest potential returns.
*   **Voting Rights:** Standard equity shares carry one vote per share.
*   **Differential Voting Rights (DVRs):** Companies like Tata Motors have issued DVR shares. These offer lower voting rights (e.g., 1 vote for every 10 shares) but typically pay a higher dividend to compensate. As of November 2025, DVRs trade at a discount to ordinary shares.

### 2. Preference Share Capital
Preference shareholders are like hybrid investors—part owner, part lender. They get priority over equity shareholders for:
*   **Dividend Payment:** They must be paid their fixed dividend before any equity dividend.
*   **Capital Repayment:** In case of bankruptcy, they get paid back before equity holders.
*   **Current Context:** Most preference shares in India are **Redeemable Preference Shares** (must be repaid within 20 years). Irredeemable preference shares are prohibited.

## Raising Capital: Real-World Examples (November 2025)

Companies raise share capital primarily through three routes. Let's look at how this is playing out in the current market cycle of FY 2025-26.

### 1. Initial Public Offering (IPO)
This is the first sale of stock by a private company to the public. It transforms "Promoter Capital" into "Public Share capital."
*   **Live Example (November 2025):** **Hero Fincorp** is scheduled to launch its IPO on **November 28, 2025**. This issuance will increase the company's paid-up capital and provide an exit for early investors. The funds raised will bolster their lending book.

### 2. Rights Issue
A company invites existing shareholders to buy additional shares, usually at a discount to the market price. This protects existing shareholders from dilution—if they subscribe.
*   **Live Example (November 2025):** **Adani Enterprises** has launched a massive **₹24,930 crore Rights Issue**, opening on **November 25, 2025**. Shareholders are offered shares at a discount. If you hold shares and don't subscribe, your percentage ownership (and share of future profits) drops. This is known as **dilution**.

### 3. Private Placement / Preferential Allotment
Selling shares to a select group of investors (like QIBs or Mutual Funds) rather than the public. This is faster but dilutes retail shareholders who don't get to participate.

## Corporate Actions Affecting Share Capital

Your number of shares can change without you buying or selling. Here is how corporate actions impact share capital:

### Bonus Issues
*   **What it is:** Free shares given to existing shareholders.
*   **Impact:** It increases the **Paid-up Capital** but *not* the cash in the company. The money is moved from "Reserves & Surplus" to "Share Capital."
*   **Example:** If a company declares a 1:1 bonus, your 100 shares become 200. The stock price typically halves, keeping your total value the same initially.

### Stock Splits
*   **What it is:** Dividing existing shares into smaller units (e.g., Face Value ₹10 to ₹2).
*   **Impact:** **No change** in Share Capital amount. Only the *number* of shares increases, and Face Value decreases. This is done to increase liquidity.

### Share Buybacks (Crucial Tax Update 2025)
*   **What it is:** The company buys its own shares from you and extinguishes them.
*   **Impact:** Reduces the Paid-up Capital and increases EPS (earnings are divided by fewer shares).
*   **Tax Warning (FY 2025-26):** As per the **Finance Act 2024** (effective October 1, 2024), the tax treatment has flipped.
    *   **Old Rule:** Company paid 20% tax; you got money tax-free.
    *   **New Rule (Nov 2025):** The entire buyback amount is taxed in **your hands** as a "Deemed Dividend" at your slab rate (up to 30% + surcharge). The cost of acquisition becomes a capital loss you can carry forward.

## Regulatory & Compliance Checklist (2025)

For investors and founders, strict regulations govern share capital to prevent fraud.

### 1. No Minimum Capital Requirement
As per the Companies (Amendment) Act, 2015, there is **no minimum paid-up capital** requirement for Private or Public Limited companies. You can technically incorporate a company with ₹100 capital, though practical norms usually see ₹1 lakh as a baseline for authorized capital.

### 2. Stamp Duty on Issuance
When shares are issued, stamp duty must be paid.
*   **Demat Mode:** As per the amended Indian Stamp Act (uniform rates), the duty on issuance of securities in demat form is **0.005%** of the value.
*   **Physical Mode:** Rates can vary by state (e.g., 0.1% in some jurisdictions), making demat issuance significantly cheaper and more efficient.

### 3. Section 56(2)(viib) "Angel Tax" Status
Investors should note that the controversial "Angel Tax" (taxing share premiums in unlisted companies) was abolished in the **Union Budget 2024**, encouraging more startup capital formation in FY 2025-26.

## Key Takeaways
*   **Hierarchy Matters:** Authorized Capital is the limit; Paid-up Capital is the actual money. Always check the gap between them to predict future dilution.
*   **Know Your Rights:** Equity shares give voting power; Preference shares give payment priority but no voting rights.
*   **Tax Alert:** Buybacks are now taxed as dividends in your hands (since Oct 2024). Factor this into your post-tax return calculations.
*   **Dilution Risk:** In a Rights Issue (like Adani Enterprises Nov 2025), failing to subscribe reduces your ownership percentage.
*   **Cost of Issuance:** Demat issuance attracts a uniform 0.005% stamp duty, incentivizing digital securities.

## FAQs on Share Capital

### What is the difference between Authorized and Paid-up Capital?
Authorized capital is the maximum value of shares a company can legally issue, as defined in its Memorandum. Paid-up capital is the actual amount received from shareholders. A company cannot issue shares exceeding its authorized limit without paying a fee to the ROC to increase it.

### How is share capital taxed during a buyback in November 2025?
Since October 1, 2024, buyback proceeds are taxed as **deemed dividends** in the hands of the shareholder at their applicable slab rates. The original cost of acquisition is treated as a capital loss, which can be set off against other capital gains.

### Can a Private Limited Company have zero share capital?
No, every company limited by shares must have some share capital. However, the **minimum requirement** (previously ₹1 lakh) was removed in 2015. A company can now be incorporated with a nominal paid-up capital, even as low as ₹100 or ₹1,000.

### What happens to share capital during a stock split?
The total value of share capital remains **unchanged**. Only the face value per share decreases, and the number of shares increases proportionately. For example, a 1:10 split changes one ₹10 share into ten ₹1 shares, but the total capital amount stays the same.

### What is the stamp duty on issuing new shares in 2025?
For shares issued in **dematerialized (demat) form**, the uniform stamp duty rate across India is **0.005%** of the issuance value. This is collected by the depository (NSDL/CDSL). Physical share issuance may attract higher state-specific rates (e.g., 0.1% in Delhi).
