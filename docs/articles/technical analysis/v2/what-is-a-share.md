## The Core Concept: What Exactly is a Share?

A share represents a unit of equity ownership in a corporation. It is a financial instrument that grants you a claim on the company's assets and earnings. In India, these are primarily traded on exchanges like the National Stock Exchange (NSE) and the Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE).

When you hold a share, you are a "shareholder." This status comes with specific rights and potential rewards, distinct from being a lender or a bondholder. Unlike a bank loan where the lender gets fixed interest regardless of the company's performance, a shareholder's returns depend entirely on the company's success.

### The Digital Transformation
Gone are the days of physical paper certificates. As of November 2025, India has over 21 crore (210 million) demat accounts, reflecting a massive shift to digital ownership. Shares today exist as electronic entries in your Demat account, held by depositories like NSDL or CDSL. This digitization ensures that when you buy a share, the transfer of ownership happens seamlessly and securely.

## How Shares Work: From IPO to Your Portfolio

Understanding the lifecycle of a share helps clarify how money moves in the market. It generally follows a two-stage process: the Primary Market and the Secondary Market.

### 1. The Primary Market (IPO)
This is where shares are born. A private company decides to go public to raise capital for expansion or debt repayment. It issues an Initial Public Offering (IPO).

*   **The Process:** You apply for shares directly from the company.
*   **The Timeline:** As per SEBI regulations effective November 1, 2025, the listing timeline is now mandatory T+3. This means if an IPO closes on Monday (T), the shares must be listed and available for trading by Thursday.

### 2. The Secondary Market
Once listed, shares trade between investors on the stock exchange. The company is no longer directly involved in these transactions. If you buy a share of Reliance Industries or TCS today, you are buying it from another investor, not from the company itself.

### 3. Settlement Cycle (T+1)
The "T" stands for Transaction Day. India operates on a standard T+1 settlement cycle.
*   **Example:** If you buy shares on Tuesday (Transaction Day), the shares will appear in your Demat account, and money will be deducted by Wednesday (T+1).
*   **Note:** As of late 2025, a T+0 (same-day) settlement cycle is also available for select stocks and brokers, allowing for even faster liquidity.

## Types of Shares in India

Not all shares are created equal. Companies issue different types to serve various purposes.

### Equity Shares (Ordinary Shares)
These are the most common shares traded on the market.
*   **Voting Rights:** One share usually equals one vote. You can vote on corporate decisions like appointing directors.
*   **Risk/Reward:** High. You get dividends only after all other obligations (like debt and preference shares) are paid.

### Preference Shares
These are a hybrid between stocks and bonds.
*   **Priority:** Preference shareholders get their dividends *before* equity shareholders. In case of bankruptcy, they are paid out before equity holders.
*   **Voting Rights:** Typically, they do not carry voting rights.
*   **Returns:** Dividends are usually fixed, similar to interest on a bond.

### DVR Shares (Differential Voting Rights)
Some companies issue shares with fewer voting rights but higher dividends to prevent dilution of control while raising capital. For example, Tata Motors has historically issued DVR shares (trading under a separate ticker) that offer higher dividends but 1/10th the voting rights of ordinary shares.

## Making Money: Capital Appreciation vs. Dividends

Investors typically look for two sources of income from shares.

### 1. Capital Appreciation
This is the profit you make when the share price increases.
*   **Example:** You buy 100 shares of a bank at ₹1,000. Two years later, the price rises to ₹1,500. Your portfolio value grows from ₹1 lakh to ₹1.5 lakh. This ₹50,000 gain is capital appreciation.

### 2. Dividends
Dividends are a portion of the company's profits distributed to shareholders. They are not mandatory; a growing company might reinvest all profits instead of paying dividends.
*   **Yield:** Mature companies (like PSUs or FMCG giants) often pay regular dividends, acting as a steady income stream.

## Tax Implications for FY 2025-26

Taxes can significantly impact your net returns. As per the Union Budget applicable for FY 2025-26, here is how your share market gains are taxed.

### Capital Gains Tax

| Type of Gain | Holding Period | Tax Rate (FY 2025-26) | Exemption Limit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Short-Term (STCG)** | Less than 12 months | **20%** flat rate | None |
| **Long-Term (LTCG)** | More than 12 months | **12.5%** flat rate | First **₹1.25 Lakh** is tax-free |

*   **Example:** If you sell shares after 15 months and make a profit of ₹2 lakh, the first ₹1.25 lakh is exempt. You pay 12.5% tax only on the remaining ₹75,000.

### Dividend Taxation
*   **Tax Rate:** Dividends are added to your total income and taxed according to your income tax slab (e.g., 30% if you are in the highest bracket).
*   **TDS Update:** As of April 1, 2025, companies deduct 10% TDS (Tax Deducted at Source) only if your total dividend income from them exceeds **₹10,000** in a financial year (limit increased from the previous ₹5,000).

## Understanding the Risks

Owning a share means owning the business risk. If the business fails, the share price can drop to zero.

*   **Market Volatility:** Prices fluctuate daily due to news, earnings, or global events. A 5-10% drop in a single day is not uncommon.
*   **Company Risk:** Specific issues like poor management, regulatory bans, or loss of market share can permanently erode value.
*   **Liquidity Risk:** In smaller companies (micro-caps), you might struggle to find a buyer when you want to sell.

## FAQs on Shares

### What is the face value of a share?
Face value is the original cost of the share listed in the company's books (often ₹1, ₹5, or ₹10). It is fixed and used to calculate dividends. It is different from the "Market Price," which fluctuates based on demand and supply.

### Can I lose more money than I invest in shares?
In the cash segment (buying shares for delivery), you cannot lose more than your invested amount. The worst-case scenario is the share price going to zero. However, in derivatives (F&O) or intraday leverage, losses can exceed your capital.

### What happens to my shares if a company goes bankrupt?
Equity shareholders are the last to be paid. If a company goes bankrupt, assets are sold to pay the government, lenders, bondholders, and preference shareholders first. Often, equity shareholders receive nothing if liabilities exceed assets.

### Do I need a broker to buy shares?
Yes, you cannot buy shares directly from the stock exchange. You must go through a SEBI-registered stockbroker like PL Capital. The broker acts as an intermediary, executing your buy and sell orders on the exchange.

### What is the difference between Nifty and Sensex?
Nifty 50 is the benchmark index of the National Stock Exchange (NSE), tracking the top 50 companies. Sensex is the benchmark of the Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE), tracking the top 30 companies. They indicate the overall market mood.
