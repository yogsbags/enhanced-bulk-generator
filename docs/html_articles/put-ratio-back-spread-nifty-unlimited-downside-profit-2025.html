Put ratio back spread suits aggressive traders expecting sharp corrections or crashes. Unlike protective puts or bear spreads, this profits from volatility expansion on downside. Understanding Put Ratio Back Spread Mechanics

What is Put Ratio Back Spread

Put ratio back spread combines puts at different strikes in specific ratio. You sell fewer higher strike puts and buy more lower strike puts.

Structure (1:2 Ratio):

  • Sell 1 put at strike A (higher strike, receive premium)
  • Buy 2 puts at strike B (lower strike, pay premium)
  • Net cost = (2 × Premium paid for B) - (1 × Premium received for A)
  • Same expiry date for all puts

This creates position with limited risk and unlimited reward. Maximum loss occurs at lower strike. Maximum gain is theoretically unlimited below lower breakeven (down to zero).

Alternative Structure (2:3 Ratio):

  • Sell 2 puts at strike A
  • Buy 3 puts at strike B
  • Maintains 1:1.5 ratio for tighter spread management

Classification:

Put ratio back spread is a volatility spread. It's also called "reverse put ratio spread." The position is vega positive (benefits from volatility increase) and has negative delta below strikes.

Strategy is neither purely bullish nor bearish. It's explosively bearish below lower breakeven with limited profit in neutral zones and defined loss at lower strike.

How Put Ratio Back Spread Works

Scenario 1: Sharp Downward Crash

Setup: Sell 1 × 24,000 put, Buy 2 × 23,800 puts. Nifty crashes to 23,200.

Your 24,000 put (short): ITM by 800 points. You owe 800 × 75 = ₹60,000. Your 23,800 puts (long): ITM by 600 points each. Worth 600 × 75 × 2 = ₹90,000. Net profit: ₹90,000 - ₹60,000 = ₹30,000 (minus any net debit paid).

Below 23,200, profit continues growing unlimited as Nifty falls toward zero.

Scenario 2: Maximum Loss Zone

Nifty expires exactly at 23,800 (your long strike).

Your 24,000 put is ITM by 200 points: Owe ₹15,000. Your 23,800 puts expire worthless: Worth ₹0. Loss: ₹15,000 + Net debit paid (if any).

This is maximum loss point. Worst case is known and limited to specific price.

Scenario 3: Range-Bound Above Strikes

Nifty expires at 24,100 (above both strikes).

All puts expire worthless. If you established for credit, you keep it. If small debit, that's your loss. Best case is market staying above strikes giving small profit from credit.

Scenario 4: Between Strikes

Nifty expires at 23,900 (between strikes).

Your 24,000 put is ITM by 100 points: Owe ₹7,500. Your 23,800 puts expire worthless. Small loss or profit depending on initial credit received.

Credit vs Debit Setup

Credit Setup (Ideal):

  • Nifty at 24,000
  • Sell 1 × 24,000 put at ₹180 (receive ₹13,500)
  • Buy 2 × 23,800 puts at ₹85 each (pay ₹12,750)
  • Net credit: ₹750

You receive money upfront. If market stays above 24,000, you keep ₹750 profit. Maximum loss ₹14,250 at 23,800.

Small Debit Setup:

  • Sell 1 × 24,000 put at ₹180
  • Buy 2 × 23,750 puts at ₹95 each (pay ₹14,250)
  • Net debit: ₹750

Small cost for unlimited downside profit. Maximum loss ₹19,500 at 23,750 (wider spread).

Step-by-Step Implementation

Step 1: Market Analysis

Identify topping patterns and potential breakdown catalysts.

Technical Assessment:

  • Price at resistance or all-time highs
  • Bearish divergence on RSI or MACD
  • Volume declining on rallies (weak upside)
  • Distribution patterns forming

Volatility Assessment:

  • India VIX below 15 (suppressed volatility)
  • Market complacent at highs
  • Historical volatility at lows
  • Fear gauge near multi-month lows

Catalyst Identification:

  • Major resistance overhead (psychological level, gap resistance)
  • Negative global setup (Fed hawkish, global slowdown)
  • Domestic concerns (fiscal deficit, inflation data)
  • Technical breakdown setup (head and shoulders, double top)

Breakdown Probability:

Study support and resistance. If Nifty at 24,200 resistance for third time with weakening momentum, breakdown likely. Put ratio back spread captures downside with unlimited profit.

Step 2: Ratio Selection

Choose ratio based on credit/debit and crash expectation.

1:2 Ratio (Most Common):

  • Sell 1 put, buy 2 puts
  • Easier to get credit or minimal debit
  • Wider profit zone below lower breakeven
  • Maximum loss at lower strike manageable

2:3 Ratio (Tighter Management):

  • Sell 2 puts, buy 3 puts
  • Requires more margin
  • Steeper profit acceleration below breakeven
  • Double the maximum loss point

1:3 Ratio (Aggressive Crash Play):

  • Sell 1 put, buy 3 puts
  • Usually costs debit
  • Massive profit on severe crashes
  • Wider lower breakeven point

Start with 1:2 ratio until comfortable with mechanics. Best balance of cost, risk, and crash profit potential.

Step 3: Strike Selection

Higher Strike (Sell): Choose ATM or slightly ITM.

If Nifty is 24,000, sell 24,000 or 24,050 put. ATM maximizes credit received. Slightly ITM increases credit but also maximum loss.

Lower Strike (Buy): Choose 100-300 points below sold strike.

For 24,000 sell, buy 23,800 or 23,700 puts. Tighter spread (100-150 points) reduces cost but increases maximum loss. Wider spread (250-300 points) may require debit but smaller maximum loss zone.

Strike Selection Rules:

  • Keep spread width 150-300 points for Nifty
  • Aim for credit or maximum ₹5,000 debit
  • Calculate maximum loss before entering (must be acceptable)
  • Lower breakeven should align with support breakdown level
  • Check liquidity on both strikes (OI 50,000+ each)
  • Buy strike should be near next major support

Step 4: Expiry Selection

Choose expiry matching catalyst timeframe and correction expectation.

Weekly Expiry (Tuesday)*:

  • Expecting sharp correction within 7-10 days
  • Lower premium cost
  • Less time for breakdown to develop
  • Higher gamma risk if market pins

Monthly Expiry:

  • 15-30 day window for topping process
  • Higher credit potential
  • More time cushion for breakdown
  • Better for technical top formations

Enter with 10-20 days to expiry for optimal setup. Avoid entering with less than 7 days (insufficient time for crash) or more than 30 days (excessive time decay cost).

Step 5: Execute the Trade

Place all legs simultaneously as complex order.

Execution Steps:

  1. Calculate exact ratio (1:2 most common)
  2. Select "Multi-leg Strategy" in platform
  3. Enter: Sell 1 × Strike A, Buy 2 × Strike B
  4. Set limit price for net credit/debit
  5. Verify margin requirement
  6. Place order

If platform doesn't support multi-leg:

  • First: Sell the higher strike puts
  • Second: Buy the lower strike puts (2× quantity) immediately
  • Third: Verify position reflects correctly

Step 6: Position Management

Rule 1: Exit on Breakdown

When Nifty breaks below lower strike with momentum, monitor closely. Exit when profit reaches 2-3× maximum loss amount or on reversal signals.

Rule 2: Defend at Maximum Loss

Market approaching your long strike with little time left? Consider rolling down both strikes or exiting to limit loss.

Rule 3: Early Exit if No Breakdown

If catalyst passes without breakdown and market stays above strikes, exit for small profit (if credit) or small loss (if debit). Don't hold hoping for late crash.

Rule 4: Time Management

With 5 days to expiry, if no breakdown developing, close position. Gamma risk at lower strike accelerates. Better to reset than risk maximum loss.

Risk and Reward Analysis

Maximum Profit Calculation

Formula: Unlimited (Down to Zero Theoretically)

Example (1:2 Ratio):

  • Sold 1 × 24,000 put at ₹180
  • Bought 2 × 23,800 puts at ₹85 each
  • Net credit: ₹750 (₹13,500 received - ₹12,750 paid)
  • Lower breakeven: 23,800 - 200 + 7.50 = 23,607.50

For every point Nifty falls below 23,607.50:

  • Short put loses ₹75 (1 contract × 75 lot)
  • Long puts gain ₹150 (2 contracts × 75 lot each)
  • Net gain: ₹75 per point

If Nifty crashes to 23,000 (607.50 points below lower breakeven): Profit = 607.50 × 75 = ₹45,562.50

If Nifty crashes to 22,000 (1,607.50 points below): Profit = 1,607.50 × 75 = ₹1,20,562.50

Unlimited downside profit is key advantage for crash protection.

Maximum Loss Calculation

Formula: (Higher Strike - Lower Strike) - Net Credit (or + Net Debit)

Maximum loss = (24,000 - 23,800) - 7.50 = 192.50 per contract For 1 spread: 192.50 × 75 = ₹14,437.50

This occurs when Nifty expires exactly at 23,800. Above this, loss decreases. Below this, position becomes profitable.

Example with Debit:

If you paid ₹2,000 net debit: Maximum loss = (200 × 75) + 2,000 = ₹17,000

Breakeven Points

Upper Breakeven: Sold Strike + Net Credit

If net credit ₹750 (₹10 per contract): Upper breakeven = 24,000 + 10 = 24,010

Above 24,010, you lose the credit. Between 24,000-24,010, you profit from credit partially.

Lower Breakeven: Bought Strike - (Spread Width - Net Credit per Contract)

Lower breakeven = 23,800 - (200 - 10) = 23,610

Below 23,610, you make unlimited profit. Between 23,800-23,610, position is recovering from maximum loss zone.

Risk-Reward Assessment

Maximum Risk: ₹14,437.50 (known and limited) Maximum Reward: Unlimited (grows linearly below lower breakeven)

If expecting 10% crash (24,000 to 21,600), potential profit = 2,000+ points × 75 × 1 net long put = ₹1,50,000+.

Risk ₹14,437 to potentially make ₹1,50,000 = 10:1+ reward-risk on crash moves. This asymmetry provides crash insurance with profit.

Understanding Risk Factors

Price Movement (Delta)

Put ratio back spread has unique delta profile. Near short strike, delta is slightly positive or neutral. Below long strike, delta turns negative and accelerates.

Practical Impact:

Small moves around current price have minimal P&L impact. Large downward moves create significant profits. This is exactly what you want - low cost for waiting, high reward for crash.

Position benefits from gamma below long strike. As Nifty falls through strikes, long puts accelerate while short put's negative gamma is offset by two long puts.

Time Decay (Theta)

Time decay impact varies by position relative to strikes.

Above Short Strike:

Theta positive (benefits you). If established for credit and market stays above, time decay adds to profit daily.

Near Long Strike (Maximum Loss Zone):

Theta most negative here. Daily decay hurts. This is why you don't want market pinned at long strike with little time remaining.

Below Long Strike:

Theta becomes less relevant. Intrinsic value dominates. You're making money from direction and volatility, not time.

Critical Period: Last 7 days, gamma and theta accelerate at lower strike. If no breakdown by then, exit position to avoid maximum loss risk.

Volatility Impact (Vega)

Volatility increase dramatically benefits ratio back spreads. This is primary profit driver besides direction on downside.

Why This Matters:

You enter when VIX is 12-14 (low complacency). Market tops, VIX spikes to 22-25 (fear). Your long puts (2×) gain massive value from volatility expansion. Short put (1×) gains less. Net vega is positive.

Even if Nifty doesn't fall much, volatility spike alone can generate 50-100% profit on the spread. Then if crash follows, profits compound dramatically.

Volatility Crush Risk:

If VIX spikes before you enter (market already falling) and then crashes, spread can lose value. Enter during low volatility at market tops, not after crash started.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Entering After Crash Started

Deploying put ratio spread when market already falling 5%. VIX spiked, premiums expensive. Works best before crash, not during.

Mistake 2: Wrong Ratio Selection

Using 1:4 or 1:5 ratios for "massive crash profit." These cost huge debit and require unrealistic crashes. Stick to 1:2 or 2:3 ratios.

Mistake 3: No Top Formation

Random entry hoping for crash. Market can rally for months. Need topping pattern or strong resistance rejection before deploying.

Mistake 4: Holding at Maximum Loss

Market pinned at your long strike, 3 days to expiry. Holding hoping for breakdown. Exit early to reduce loss from maximum.

Mistake 5: Ignoring Lower Breakeven

Thinking any decline is profitable. Forgetting you need move below lower breakeven for unlimited profit. Calculate and mark this level clearly.

Mistake 6: Over-Deployment

Setting 3-4 put ratio back spreads simultaneously. One hits maximum loss, multiple losses compound. Limit to 1 spread per setup.

Mistake 7: Fighting Bull Markets

Deploying bearish strategies in strong uptrends. Low probability of success. Wait for clear top formation and distribution before entering.
