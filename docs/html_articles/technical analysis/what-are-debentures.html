Debentures act as a loan you provide to a company. Unlike shareholders who own a piece of the business, debenture holders are creditors who earn regular interest income. For Indian investors in 2025, these instruments have become significantly more accessible. In July 2024, SEBI reduced the minimum face value of privately placed debt securities to ₹10,000, breaking down the high entry barrier that previously kept retail investors out of the corporate bond market. Whether you are looking for steady cash flow or an alternative to Fixed Deposits (FDs), understanding debentures is essential for modern wealth management.

Understanding the Meaning of Debentures

A debenture is a debt instrument used by companies to borrow money from the public or institutional investors for a defined period at a fixed interest rate. When you buy a debenture, you are essentially lending money to the issuer (the company). In return, the issuer promises to repay the principal amount at maturity and pay interest (coupon) at regular intervals—monthly, quarterly, or annually.

Core Characteristics

  • Nature: It is a loan (debt), not ownership (equity).
  • Coupon Rate: The fixed interest rate paid to investors. For example, recent NCD issues in late 2025 offered coupon rates ranging from 9% to 10.5%.
  • Tenure: They come with a fixed maturity period, which can range from 1 year to 10+ years.
  • Credit Rating: Agencies like CRISIL, ICRA, and CARE assign ratings (e.g., AAA, AA+) to indicate the safety of the instrument. A higher rating implies lower default risk.

Why Companies Issue Them

Companies prefer issuing debentures over equity because it does not dilute ownership. Existing shareholders retain control, while the company gets the necessary capital for expansion or working capital. For investors, debentures offer higher potential returns than traditional savings instruments like bank FDs, though with higher risk.

Different Types of Debentures in India

In the Indian market, debentures are categorized based on security, convertibility, and redeemability. Understanding these distinctions is vital for risk management.

1. Based on Security

  • Secured Debentures: These are backed by specific company assets (like land, buildings, or machinery). If the company defaults, these assets can be sold to repay investors. In India, most public issues of NCDs are secured to protect retail investors.
  • Unsecured Debentures: These are not backed by collateral. They rely solely on the creditworthiness and reputation of the issuer. To compensate for the higher risk, they typically offer higher interest rates.

2. Based on Convertibility

  • Convertible Debentures: These can be converted into equity shares of the company after a specific period. The conversion ratio is decided at the time of issuance.
  • Non-Convertible Debentures (NCDs): These cannot be converted into shares. They remain pure debt instruments until maturity. NCDs are the most popular form of debenture investment for retail investors in India due to their high fixed yields.

3. Based on Redeemability

  • Redeemable Debentures: The company repays the principal amount on a specific maturity date.
  • Perpetual (Irredeemable) Debentures: These have no fixed maturity date. The company pays interest indefinitely, and the principal is usually repaid only if the company winds up or chooses to exercise a "call option" to buy them back. (Note: These are complex and carry higher risk, often classified as AT1 bonds in the banking sector).

Taxation on Debentures in India

Taxation rules for debt instruments underwent significant changes in the July 2024 Budget. As of November 2025, here is how your debenture income is taxed for FY 2025-26 (AY 2026-27).

1. Interest Income

Interest earned from debentures is added to your total income and taxed according to your applicable income tax slab rates. This applies to both listed and unlisted debentures.

  • TDS (Tax Deducted at Source): As per Section 193 of the Income Tax Act, companies deduct 10% TDS on interest payments for listed debentures if the amount exceeds ₹5,000 (for individuals). You can claim this back when filing your ITR if your total tax liability is lower.

2. Capital Gains Tax

The tax treatment depends on whether the debenture is listed or unlisted and the holding period.

A. Listed Debentures (Traded on NSE/BSE)

  • Holding Period: 12 months.
  • Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG): If sold within 12 months, gains are added to your income and taxed at your slab rate.
  • Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG): If held for more than 12 months, gains are taxed at 12.5% without indexation benefits. (Note: The indexation benefit was removed for listed bonds/debentures in the Finance Act 2024).

B. Unlisted Debentures

  • Tax Treatment: As per the Finance Act 2024, gains from unlisted debentures are deemed Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG) irrespective of the holding period. They are taxed at your applicable slab rate.

C. Market Linked Debentures (MLDs)

  • Tax Treatment: As per Section 50AA, gains from MLDs are always treated as Short-Term Capital Gains, taxed at your slab rate, regardless of how long you hold them.

Advantages and Disadvantages of Investing in Debentures

Before allocating capital, weigh the potential rewards against the inherent risks.

Advantages

  1. Higher Fixed Returns: NCDs typically offer interest rates of 9-11%_, which is significantly higher than bank FDs (typically 6.5-7.5% as of late 2025). _(Rates vary by issuer credit rating).
  2. Regular Income: They provide predictable cash flow, making them suitable for retirees or those needing monthly/annual payouts.
  3. Liquidity (Listed NCDs): You can sell listed NCDs on the secondary market (NSE/BSE) before maturity if you need funds, though trading volumes can vary.
  4. No Dilution: Unlike equity, debentures do not dilute the ownership stake of existing shareholders.

Disadvantages

  1. Credit Risk (Default Risk): The biggest risk is the company defaulting on interest or principal payments. History has seen defaults even from rated companies (e.g., DHFL, IL&FS crisis). Always check the credit rating (AAA is safest).
  2. Interest Rate Risk: If market interest rates rise, the price of existing fixed-rate debentures falls. If you sell before maturity, you might incur a capital loss.
  3. Tax Efficiency: Interest income is fully taxable at slab rates. Unlike equity funds, debt instruments no longer enjoy indexation benefits, reducing their post-tax returns for those in the 30% tax bracket.
  4. Liquidity Risk: While listed, some NCDs trade very thinly. You might not find a buyer immediately at the fair price if you wish to exit early.

Key Takeaways

  • Debt, Not Equity: Debentures are loans to companies, offering fixed interest payouts.
  • New Accessibility: SEBI's reduction of the face value to ₹10,000 (July 2024) makes private placement NCDs accessible to smaller investors.
  • Tax Impact: Listed NCDs held for >12 months attract 12.5% LTCG tax. Interest is always taxed at your slab rate.
  • Check Ratings: Never invest without checking the credit rating (CRISIL, ICRA, CARE). stick to AA and AAA ratings for safety.
  • Secured is Safer: Prioritize secured NCDs over unsecured ones to ensure you have a claim on assets in case of default.

FAQs on Debentures

What is a Debenture in Simple Terms?

A debenture is simply a loan you give to a large company. Instead of borrowing from a bank, the company borrows from you and thousands of other investors. In exchange, they promise to pay you a fixed interest rate regularly and return your original money after a set number of years.

What is a Debenture vs Bond?

In India, the terms are often used interchangeably, but there is a technical difference. Bonds are typically issued by the Government or Public Sector Undertakings (PSUs) and are often unsecured but backed by sovereign guarantee. Debentures are issued by private companies and are usually secured by company assets to provide safety to investors.

What is the Difference Between a Debenture and a Share?

Shares represent ownership; when you buy a share, you own a part of the company and share in its profits (dividends) or losses. Debentures represent debt; you are a lender, not an owner. You get fixed interest regardless of whether the company makes a profit or a loss, but you have no voting rights.

Is a Debenture a Loan?

Yes, from the company's perspective, a debenture is a loan. It appears as a liability (debt) on the company's balance sheet. For the investor, it is a lending instrument. Unlike a bank loan taken from a single entity, a debenture issue splits the loan into smaller units (e.g., ₹1,000 or ₹10,000) that many retail investors can buy.

Can I sell my debentures before maturity?

Yes, if the debentures are listed on a stock exchange like NSE or BSE, you can sell them in the secondary market before the maturity date. However, liquidity depends on market demand. If trading volumes are low, you might have to sell at a discount to the fair value.
