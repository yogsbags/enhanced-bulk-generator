# What is ETF?

## Article Structure

### H1: Exchange Traded Funds – What is ETF & How to Invest

**Introductory Content:**
- Start the paragraph with "ETF"
- Definition: Exchange Traded Fund - mutual fund that trades like a stock
- Combines benefits of stocks (intraday trading) + mutual funds (diversification)
- Why it matters for Indian investors: Low-cost passive investing, real-time liquidity
- Context: Growing ETF market in India (₹7 lakh crore AUM, Nov 2025)
- Listed on NSE/BSE, requires Demat account to trade
- SEBI-regulated, transparent holdings updated daily

### H2: Meaning of ETF

- **Definition**: Exchange Traded Fund - basket of securities traded on stock exchange
- Passively managed to track an index (Nifty 50, Sensex, Gold, etc.)
- Each ETF unit represents fractional ownership of underlying assets
- **Key Difference from Mutual Funds**:
  - ETF trades throughout market hours at fluctuating prices
  - Mutual fund trades once daily at fixed NAV
- **Indian Examples**:
  - Nippon India ETF Nifty BeES (tracks Nifty 50)
  - SBI ETF Nifty 50 (tracks Nifty 50)
  - HDFC Gold ETF (tracks gold prices)
  - Bharat Bond ETF (tracks government bonds)
- **Price Mechanism**: Market demand/supply (may trade at premium/discount to NAV)
- **Creation/Redemption**: Through Authorized Participants (APs) in large blocks

### H2: How Do ETFs Work?

- **Step 1: ETF Creation**:
  - AMC (Asset Management Company) creates ETF scheme
  - Authorized Participant (AP) provides basket of securities to AMC
  - AMC issues ETF units in exchange (Creation Units, typically 50,000 units)
- **Step 2: Listing on Exchange**:
  - ETF units listed on NSE/BSE
  - Available for retail investors to buy/sell like stocks
- **Step 3: Intraday Trading**:
  - Investors buy/sell ETF units during market hours (9:15 AM - 3:30 PM)
  - Price fluctuates based on supply/demand (may differ from NAV)
- **Step 4: Tracking Index**:
  - ETF portfolio mirrors underlying index (Nifty 50, Sensex)
  - No active stock picking, purely passive replication
- **Step 5: Redemption**:
  - Large investors (APs) can redeem Creation Units for underlying securities
  - Retail investors sell units on exchange (no direct redemption)
- **NAV vs Market Price**:
  - NAV = True value of underlying assets
  - Market Price = Trading price on exchange (can be ±0.1% of NAV)
  - Arbitrage opportunity if price deviates significantly from NAV

### H2: Types of ETFs

**Give pointers and make them H3:**

#### H3: Equity ETFs
- Track stock market indices (Nifty 50, Sensex, Nifty Bank, Nifty IT)
- Provide instant diversification across 30-50 stocks
- Indian Examples:
  - Nippon India ETF Nifty BeES (Nifty 50)
  - ICICI Prudential Nifty Next 50 ETF (Next 50 large caps)
  - UTI Nifty 50 ETF
- Suitable for long-term equity exposure

#### H3: Gold ETFs
- Track domestic gold prices (99.5% purity)
- Each unit = 1 gram of gold (or fraction thereof)
- No storage hassle, lower cost than physical gold
- Indian Examples:
  - HDFC Gold ETF
  - Nippon India Gold BeES
  - SBI Gold ETF
- Taxed like physical gold (LTCG 20% with indexation if held >36 months)

#### H3: Debt ETFs (Bond ETFs)
- Track bond indices or government securities
- Fixed income with lower risk than equity
- Indian Examples:
  - Bharat Bond ETF (PSU bonds, fixed maturity)
  - Nippon India ETF Long Term Gilt
  - CPSE Bond ETF (Central PSU bonds)
- Suitable for conservative investors seeking debt exposure

#### H3: International ETFs
- Provide exposure to foreign markets (US, global indices)
- Currency-hedged or unhedged options
- Indian Examples:
  - Motilal Oswal S&P 500 ETF (US market exposure)
  - Nippon India ETF Hang Seng BeES (Hong Kong)
  - Not many international ETFs in India (mostly feeder funds)
- Helps global diversification

#### H3: Sector/Thematic ETFs
- Focus on specific sectors or themes
- Indian Examples:
  - Nippon India ETF Nifty Bank BeES (banking sector)
  - ICICI Prudential Nifty IT ETF (IT sector)
  - Motilal Oswal Nifty Midcap 150 ETF
- Higher concentration risk than broad index ETFs

#### H3: Commodity ETFs
- Track prices of commodities (gold, silver)
- Gold ETFs most popular in India
- Silver ETFs available but less liquid
- Other commodity ETFs (oil, agricultural) not yet available in India

### H2: Benefits of Investing in ETFs

- **Low Expense Ratio**: 0.05% - 0.25% (vs 1-2% for active mutual funds)
  - Example: Nifty 50 ETF charges 0.05% vs Nifty 50 Index Fund 0.20%
- **Intraday Trading Flexibility**: Buy/sell anytime during market hours
  - Exit positions quickly if needed (unlike mutual funds with T+2 settlement)
- **Transparency**: Holdings disclosed daily (matches underlying index)
  - No surprises, no style drift
- **Tax Efficiency**: Lower portfolio turnover = fewer capital gains events
  - Equity ETF: LTCG 12.5% (>12 months), STCG 20% (<12 months)
  - Same as equity mutual funds
- **Diversification**: Single transaction gives exposure to 30-500 stocks
  - Nifty 50 ETF = Instant exposure to India's top 50 companies
- **Liquidity**: Trade on NSE/BSE with tight bid-ask spreads
  - Popular ETFs have high volumes (Nifty BeES, SBI Nifty ETF)
- **Lower Tracking Error**: Closely mirrors index performance
  - Tracking difference typically 0.05-0.10% annually
- **No Fund Manager Risk**: Passive strategy, no subjective decisions
- **Fractional Ownership**: Buy even 1 unit (₹100-200 typically)

### H2: Risks of ETFs

- **Tracking Error**: ETF performance may deviate slightly from index
  - Due to expense ratio, rebalancing costs, cash drag
- **Bid-Ask Spread**: Difference between buy and sell price
  - Low-volume ETFs have wider spreads, eating into returns
  - Example: Buy at ₹100.50, sell at ₹100.00 = ₹0.50 loss
- **Liquidity Risk**: Some ETFs have low trading volumes
  - Difficult to exit large positions without impacting price
  - Stick to popular ETFs (Nifty BeES, SBI Nifty 50, HDFC Gold)
- **Market Risk**: Equity ETFs subject to stock market volatility
  - Not immune to market crashes
- **Premium/Discount to NAV**: ETF may trade above/below true value
  - Usually ±0.2%, but can widen in volatile markets
- **No SIP Facility**: Most brokers don't offer automatic SIP in ETFs
  - Need to manually buy every month (vs auto-debit in mutual funds)
- **Intraday Temptation**: Easy to overtrade, incurring brokerage costs
  - Long-term investors should resist intraday trading urges
- **Demat Account Mandatory**: Cannot invest without Demat (unlike some MFs)

### H2: How to Invest in ETF?

**Step 1: Open Demat & Trading Account**
- Choose broker (Zerodha, Upstox, ICICI Direct, PL Capital)
- Complete KYC (Aadhaar + PAN)
- Link bank account for fund transfer

**Step 2: Research & Select ETF**
- Decide objective: Equity exposure, gold, debt, international
- Check:
  - Expense ratio (lower is better)
  - Average daily trading volume (higher is better)
  - Tracking error (lower is better)
  - AUM size (₹500 crore+ preferred)
- Indian Recommendation: Nifty 50 ETF for equity, Gold ETF for hedge

**Step 3: Check Liquidity & Spreads**
- Look at bid-ask spread (should be <0.2%)
- Check average daily volume (>10 lakh units for popular ETFs)
- Avoid illiquid ETFs with <1 lakh daily volume

**Step 4: Place Order on Exchange**
- Open trading platform (Kite, Upstox, etc.)
- Search for ETF ticker (e.g., "NIFTYBEES" for Nippon Nifty ETF)
- Choose order type:
  - Market Order: Executes immediately at current price
  - Limit Order: Executes only at specified price or better
- Enter quantity and confirm

**Step 5: Settlement & Holding**
- ETF units credited to Demat account (T+1 settlement)
- Hold for long-term wealth creation
- Track performance vs index (should match closely)

**Step 6: Monitor & Rebalance (Optional)**
- Review holdings quarterly
- Rebalance portfolio if allocation drifts
- For most investors: Buy and hold is sufficient

**Manual SIP Workaround:**
- Set monthly reminder
- Manually buy fixed quantity each month
- Some brokers offer "ETF Basket" for automated purchases

### H2: FAQs on ETFs

#### H3: Is ETF Better Than Mutual Fund?

- **Neither is universally "better"** - depends on your needs:
- **ETF Advantages**:
  - Lower expense ratio (0.05% vs 0.5-2% for MF)
  - Intraday trading flexibility
  - Tighter tracking to index (lower tracking error)
  - Transparent holdings (daily disclosure)
- **Mutual Fund Advantages**:
  - SIP facility widely available (automated monthly investing)
  - No Demat account needed for many funds
  - Can buy directly from AMC or platforms
  - Better for systematic, disciplined investing
- **Verdict**: ETF for lump sum + low cost, Mutual Fund (Index Fund) for SIP convenience

#### H3: Which ETF Is Best in India?

- **Best Equity ETFs**:
  - **Nippon India ETF Nifty BeES** (Nifty 50 tracker, oldest, most liquid)
  - **SBI ETF Nifty 50** (Low expense ratio 0.07%, high volume)
  - **HDFC Nifty 50 ETF** (Tight tracking, good liquidity)
- **Best Gold ETF**:
  - **HDFC Gold ETF** (Highest AUM, good liquidity)
  - **Nippon India Gold BeES** (Low expense ratio)
- **Best Debt ETF**:
  - **Bharat Bond ETF** (PSU bonds, fixed maturity 2025/2030/2031)
  - **CPSE Bond ETF** (Central PSU bonds)
- **Selection Criteria**: Low expense ratio + high trading volume + tight tracking

#### H3: Is ETF a Good Investment?

- **Yes, for specific investor profiles**:
  - **Good for**:
    - Long-term passive investors (10+ years)
    - Lump sum investors (bonus, inheritance, sale proceeds)
    - Cost-conscious investors (want lowest expense ratio)
    - Traders needing intraday flexibility
  - **Not ideal for**:
    - SIP investors (manual process, not automated)
    - Beginners without Demat account
    - Those seeking active management / alpha generation
- **Performance**: ETFs match index performance (Nifty 50 ETF ≈ Nifty 50 returns minus 0.05-0.10%)
- **Long-term Track Record**: 80% of active funds fail to beat Nifty 50 over 10 years, making ETFs smart choice

#### H3: Are ETFs 100% Safe?

- **No, ETFs are NOT 100% safe** (market-linked investments have risk):
- **Market Risk**: Equity ETFs fall when stock market crashes
  - Example: Nifty 50 ETF fell 35% during March 2020 COVID crash
- **Tracking Error Risk**: May not perfectly match index
- **Liquidity Risk**: Low-volume ETFs hard to exit at fair price
- **Currency Risk**: International ETFs exposed to rupee-dollar fluctuations
- **Safest ETF Type**: Gilt ETFs (government bond ETFs) have lowest risk
  - But still not "100% safe" (interest rate risk, inflation risk)
- **Safety Spectrum**: Liquid Funds > Debt ETFs > Gold ETFs > Equity ETFs
- **SEBI Protection**: ETFs regulated by SEBI, holdings transparent, but returns not guaranteed
