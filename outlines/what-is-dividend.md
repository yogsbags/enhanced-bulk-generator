# What is Dividend?

## Article Structure

### H1: What is Dividend? Types, Taxation & Best Dividend Stocks in India

**Introductory Content:**
- Start the paragraph with "Dividend"
- Definition: Share of company profits distributed to shareholders
- Two forms: Cash dividend (money) or Stock dividend (additional shares)
- Why it matters for Indian investors: Passive income, sign of financial health
- Context: Indian companies paid ₹2.5 lakh crore in dividends (FY 2024-25)
- Regulated by Companies Act 2013, taxed under TDS provisions
- Popular among retirees and income-seeking investors

### H2: Understanding the Meaning of Dividend

- **Definition**: Portion of company's earnings paid to shareholders
- **Board Declaration**: Board of Directors decides dividend amount and frequency
- **Payment Frequency**:
  - Annual dividend (once a year, after AGM approval)
  - Interim dividend (mid-year, declared by Board without shareholder approval)
  - Quarterly dividend (rare in India, common in US)
- **Dividend Per Share (DPS)**: Amount paid per share held
  - Example: TCS declares ₹25 dividend → Shareholder with 100 shares gets ₹2,500
- **Types of Dividends**:
  - Cash Dividend: Money credited to bank account
  - Stock Dividend (Bonus Shares): Additional shares issued (e.g., 1:1 bonus)
  - Special Dividend: One-time extra payment (excess cash distribution)
  - Property Dividend: Rare, distribution of assets instead of cash
- **Indian Context**: Most Indian companies pay annual dividend after financial year-end

### H2: How Dividends Work

- **Step 1: Company Earns Profits**
  - After expenses, taxes, reserves, company has distributable profits
- **Step 2: Board Declares Dividend**
  - Board proposes dividend per share amount
  - Annual General Meeting (AGM) approves dividend (for final dividend)
  - Interim dividend doesn't need shareholder approval
- **Step 3: Record Date & Ex-Dividend Date**
  - **Record Date**: Only shareholders on this date get dividend
  - **Ex-Dividend Date**: T-1 day before record date (buy before this to get dividend)
  - Example: Record date = Dec 10 → Ex-date = Dec 9 → Buy by Dec 8 to qualify
- **Step 4: Dividend Payment**
  - Paid within 30 days of declaration (for interim) or AGM approval (for final)
  - Credited to bank account linked to Demat
- **Step 5: TDS Deduction**
  - Company deducts TDS @ 10% (if total dividend >₹5,000 in FY)
  - Net amount credited to shareholder

### H2: How Do Dividends Affect a Stock's Share Price?

- **Price Adjustment on Ex-Dividend Date**:
  - Stock price typically falls by dividend amount on ex-date
  - Example: Stock trading at ₹1,000, declares ₹50 dividend → Opens at ₹950 on ex-date
  - This is mechanical adjustment, not value destruction
- **Indian Example: Reliance Industries**
  - Declares ₹10 dividend, stock price ₹2,800
  - Ex-dividend date: Stock opens at ₹2,790 (₹10 adjustment)
  - Over time, price recovers based on fundamentals
- **Short-term vs Long-term Impact**:
  - Short-term: Price drops by dividend amount (tax-adjusted)
  - Long-term: High-quality dividend stocks recover and appreciate
- **Dividend Capture Strategy**:
  - Buy before ex-date, sell after dividend (not recommended due to taxes and price adjustment)

### H2: Why Do Companies Pay Dividends?

- **Signal of Financial Health**: Consistent dividends show profitability and cash flow strength
  - Example: TCS, Infosys, HUL pay regular dividends (blue-chip dividend payers)
- **Reward Shareholders**: Share profits with owners of the company
  - Especially important for retail investors seeking income
- **Attract Investors**: Dividend-paying stocks appeal to conservative, income-seeking investors
  - Pension funds, retirees prefer dividend stocks over growth stocks
- **Mature Business Indicator**: Companies with limited growth opportunities return cash
  - Example: Utility companies, FMCG companies with stable cash flows
- **Tax Efficiency for Company**: Dividend distribution tax (DDT) abolished in 2020
  - Now taxed in hands of shareholder (TDS 10%), making it more tax-efficient for companies
- **Alternative to Buybacks**: Some companies prefer dividends over share buybacks
  - Buybacks benefit all shareholders, dividends benefit only those holding on record date

### H2: Are Dividends Irrelevant?

- **Dividend Irrelevance Theory (Modigliani-Miller)**:
  - Total shareholder return = Capital gains + Dividends
  - Whether company pays dividend or retains cash, total value remains same
  - Example: ₹100 stock pays ₹10 dividend → Price adjusts to ₹90 → Net value unchanged
- **Practical Reality - Dividends ARE Relevant**:
  - **Tax Implications**: Dividends taxed @ 10% TDS, capital gains taxed @ 12.5% LTCG
  - **Investor Preference**: Many investors prefer cash income over selling shares
  - **Market Signal**: Dividend cuts signal trouble, increases signal confidence
  - **Behavioral Finance**: Investors value "bird in hand" (dividend) over uncertain future gains
- **Indian Context - Dividends Matter**:
  - Retirees and HNIs prefer dividend-paying stocks (Nifty Dividend Opportunities 50 index exists)
  - SEBI regulations encourage consistent dividend policy
- **Verdict**: Theoretically irrelevant, practically very relevant for investor behavior and taxation

### H2: Dividends in Financial Modeling

- **Dividend Discount Model (DDM)**: Values stock based on present value of future dividends
  - Formula: Stock Value = D1 / (r - g)
  - D1 = Expected dividend next year, r = required return, g = dividend growth rate
- **Use in Valuation**: Suitable for mature, dividend-paying companies
  - Example: Utility companies, FMCG stocks, PSU banks
  - Not suitable for growth stocks (no/low dividends)
- **Gordon Growth Model**: Assumes constant dividend growth
  - Indian Example: HUL grows dividends @ 10% annually → Model estimates fair value
- **Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE)**: Dividend capacity = FCFE
  - If dividends < FCFE → Company can afford to pay more
  - If dividends > FCFE → Unsustainable, paying from reserves

### H2: FAQs on Dividend

#### H3: What Does 5% Dividend Mean?

- **Dividend Yield of 5%**: Annual dividend equals 5% of current stock price
- **Example Calculation**:
  - Stock price: ₹1,000
  - Annual dividend per share: ₹50
  - Dividend yield = (₹50 / ₹1,000) × 100 = 5%
- **Indian Example**: ITC trades at ₹400, pays ₹20 dividend annually → 5% yield
- **Income Perspective**: ₹1 lakh investment generates ₹5,000 annual dividend income
- **Comparison**: 5% dividend yield > FD rate (6-7%), but FD is guaranteed while dividend is subject to company performance
- **Sustainability Check**: Ensure payout ratio <60% to confirm dividend is sustainable

#### H3: What Are Dividend Stocks?

#### H3: Are Dividends Free Money?

- **No, dividends are NOT free money**:
- **Stock Price Adjusts**: On ex-dividend date, price drops by dividend amount
  - Example: ₹1,000 stock pays ₹50 dividend → Price adjusts to ₹950
  - Net wealth unchanged (₹950 stock + ₹50 cash = ₹1,000)
- **Already Your Money**: Dividend is company's profit, which belongs to shareholders
  - Paying dividend = Converting part of stock value to cash
- **Tax Implications**: Dividends taxed @ 10% TDS (plus slab rate if total income high)
  - Capital gains taxed @ 12.5% LTCG (if held >12 months)
  - Dividend may be less tax-efficient than selling shares in some cases
- **Benefit**: Provides cash flow without selling shares (psychological benefit)
- **Verdict**: Dividend is return of your own capital, not "free" money, but useful for income generation
